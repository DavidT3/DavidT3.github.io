Dr David J Turner

| Research Associate | Michigan State University | turne540@msu.edu |

This is the personal website of Dr David J Turner, an astrophysicist and open source software developer, currently a research associate working at Michigan State University in the Physics and Astronomy department with Professor Megan Donahue. I am currently a member of the Local Volume Complete Cluster Survey (LoVoCCS), the X-ray Cluster Survey (XCS; formerly the XMM Cluster Survey), the Dark Energy Survey (DES), and the LSST-DESC collaboration.

My primary scientific interest/expertise is currently focused on X-ray astronomy and multi-wavelength analyses of galaxy clusters to provide constraints on their mass, temperature, density, and the relations between these properties. Not only does this tell us a lot about individual clusters, but the scatter of the total mass with respect to these different properties must be understood to successfully measure cosmology using galaxy clusters. Other research interests include active galaxies, and low-metallicity, star-forming galaxies known as 'Peas'. I am also interested in very large scale analyses of sources, in order to search for unusual or discrepant behaviours; this comes from my time spent working as a part of XCS, a serendipitous survey of galaxy clusters. I would be surprised if there weren't objects of a type previously unknown to us in the large archives of telescopes like XMM, and so I work on software that makes these large scale analyses easier, as the scale of the data we can search is only ever going to increase. My research is enabled by the open-source Python X-ray astronomy modules XGA and DAXA; I am the founder and lead developer, managing the addition of new features. This is part of my mission to democratise astronomy specialisms through easy to use software.

If you would like to discuss any of my research, software tools, or invite me to give a talk at your institution about galaxy clusters, X-ray astronomy, or open-source science/software please feel free to send me an email at turne540@msu.edu; new ideas for collaborations are also always welcome!

Ongoing Research Projects

X-ray Local Volume Complete Cluster Survey (X-LoVoCCS)

I am the X-ray lead for the LoVoCCS collaboration, using archival X-ray observations to probe the the ICM (measuring temperature, metallicity, gas/total mass, etc.), complementing the extraordinary 2D weak-lensing mass maps being measured. This project involves making the X-ray analysis entirely reproducible and open; read more in the the LoVoCCS collaboration repository.

Hydrostatic Masses of SDSS Selected Galaxy Clusters

This involves the measurement of hydrostatic masses (and thus temperature and hot baryon density radial profiles) for ~120 galaxy clusters observed by the XMM-Newton X-ray telescope, but selected from the SDSS redMaPPer catalogue. These clusters are a subset of the SDSSRM-XCS sample created by Paul Giles for XCS. This project makes use of X-ray: Generate and Analyse (XGA).

XMM Analysis of LOFAR (LoLSS/LoTSS) radio sources

I am leveraging XGA's ability to easily analyse X-ray data for large samples to produce an X-ray property catalogue for LOFAR selected objects, with upper-limits for non-detections. It will be reproducible as XGA is open-source, and a 'cookbook' for analysing LOFAR sources with XGA will be included. This catalogue is intended as a resource for the community, with myraid scientific uses; my initial focus is on using high-dimensional clustering of X-ray/radio characteristics to search for unusual sources.

Searching for Pea Galaxies and Examining Their X-ray Emission

Pea galaxies are curious objects, a possible analogue for high-redshift galaxies, they are minimally enriched and have significant star formation. I am studying their X-ray emission, in order to probe whether they have an enhanced X-ray luminosity over the value expected from their SFR. The sample sizes are still very limited, so the first step is finding more of them. I am achieving this through the use of the SDSS database and an ensemble of machine learning techniques.

Mass-Observable Relations for DES and ACT Clusters

As a member of the Dark Energy Survey, I am using XGA to measure spatially resolved ICM properties for galaxy clusters selected from DES and ACT that have archival XMM observations. This is a continuation of my work on hydrostatic masses of SDSSRM-XCS clusters, and the combined sample of hydrostatic masses is the largest ever measured. The overarching goal is the calibration of mass-observable relation normalisation and scatter, to enable cluster cosmology efforts.

Open Source Astrophysics Software

X-ray: Generate and Analyse (XGA)

An open source Python package for generalised X-ray analysis, with current support for XMM-Newton and eROSITA Calibration and Performance Verification data (more to come). XGA greatly simplifies X-ray analyses for both individual objects and large samples, providing comprehensive X-ray data product generation/analysis abilities through a Python interface. XGA uses a 'source based' paradigm, where different Python classes represent different types of astrophysical object, and the user only needs to pass a position to start their exploration.

Democratising Archival X-ray Astronomy (DAXA)

An open source Python package for the acquisition of X-ray data and creation of multi-mission X-ray archives, for use with XGA and other analysis software. All X-ray observations from multiple telescopes can be downloaded and processed, or only those relevant to a user's work. All data are filtered and downloaded through a simple Python interface, and all telescopes can be searched simultaneously. Currently DAXA can download data from XMM, Chandra, eROSITA, NuSTAR, Swift, ROSAT, Suzaku, ASCA, and INTEGRAL archives; only XMM data can be fully processed.
